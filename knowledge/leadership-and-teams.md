# Leadership & Teams — Ultra Hardcore Work

## HIRING

### Look for Evidence of Exceptional Ability
"When hiring, I look for evidence of exceptional ability, or at least exceptional aspiration."

Interview method: "I ask people to tell me the story of their career and some of the tougher problems they dealt with, how they dealt with those, and how they made decisions at key transition points."

The test: "The person who had to struggle with the problem really understands it and they don't forget if it was difficult. Ask them detailed questions — they'll know the answers. A person who was not responsible for that accomplishment will not know the details."

### Don't Over-Index on Credentials
"I sometimes wonder, 'If Nikola Tesla applied to Tesla, would we even give him an interview?' They might say, 'This guy came from some weird college in Eastern Europe.' Our response should be, 'This kid's super smart. What does he want? We'll pay him anything!'"

"Pay close attention to people who haven't completed their grad or even undergrad, but are obviously brilliant. Better to have them join before they achieve a breakthrough."

### Character Matters
"I've made several hiring decisions where I valued intellect over heart and I think that was a mistake."

"Look at the character of their friends and associates. While people can put up a mask themselves, their friends and associates will not."

### Attitude Over Skills
"When hiring, look for people with the right attitude. Skills can be taught. Attitude changes require a brain transplant."

### Recruit Aggressively
When recruiting a key engineer for SpaceX, Elon called Google to get the engineer's wife transferred to LA so there was no reason to refuse the offer.

## THE "SPECIAL FORCES" APPROACH

### Only Exceptional Is Passing
"My philosophy for companies in the startup phase is a 'Special Forces' approach. The minimum passing grade is excellent."

"A small group of technically strong people will always beat a large group of moderately strong people."

"Wherever the smartest, most driven people are choosing to work, that company is going to win."

### Money Is Not the Constraint
"If there was a factory producing excellent engineers, that would be true. Except this factory doesn't exist. It's incredibly difficult to find the right talent, integrate them into an organization, and have it work effectively."

"The fundamental limitation is exceptional engineers. There are not that many."

### Attract Through Mission
"Having a strong sense of purpose will attract the very best talent in the world. If the work is enjoyable, the financial rewards are good, and the product will change the world — that's a pretty powerful set of motivators."

## ORGANIZATIONAL DESIGN

### Remove Boundaries
"A major source of issues is poor communication between departments."

The chain of command problem: "If, to get something done, an individual contributor has to talk to their manager, who talks to a director, who talks to a VP, who talks to another VP... superdumb things will happen."

"Anyone can and should talk to anyone else according to what they think is the fastest way to solve a problem. You can talk to your manager's manager without permission. You can talk directly to a VP in another department. You can talk to me."

"Any manager who attempts to enforce chain of command communication will soon find themselves working elsewhere."

### Work for the Company, Not Your Department
"How can it possibly help Tesla for departments to erect barriers between themselves? We are all in the same boat."

"You should consider yourself obligated to [talk directly to anyone] until the right thing happens."

### Connect Design, Engineering, and Manufacturing
"You cannot separate design, engineering, and manufacturing. They need to be together."

"The people on the assembly line should be able to immediately grab a designer or engineer and say, 'WHY DID YOU MAKE IT THIS WAY!?'"

The stove analogy: "If your hand is on a stove and it gets hot, you pull it right off. But if it's someone else's hand, it will take you longer. Let the manufacturers put the designers' hands on the stove too."

### Product Reflects Org Structure
"In any product, you can see the errors in the organization's structure." Tesla Model 3 had a battery cover AND a car underbody on top of it — because two teams each thought they needed an enclosure. Delete the redundant part.

## FRONTLINE LEADERSHIP

### Sleep on the Factory Floor
"If there was a crisis, I slept on the floor. Not in the conference room — on the factory floor, where people could see me."

"When the team is being asked to work super hard, I have to be right there. If I fall asleep at four in the morning and wake up four hours later, they see that."

### Lead Like Napoleon
"Think about war. Do you want the general in some ivory tower or on the front lines? Nobody bleeds for the prince in the palace."

"Wherever Napoleon was, that's where his armies would be at their best."

### No Executive Privileges
"We eliminate all special privileges of executives. Everyone has equal access to parking, eating at the same tables, no management offices."

"Managers should always take care of their team before they take care of themselves. The supervisor is there to serve his team, not the other way round."

### Technical Managers Must Do the Work
"All technical managers must spend at least 20% of their time coding [or equivalent]. Solar roof managers must spend time on roofs. Otherwise, they are like a cavalry leader who can't ride a horse."

### Go to the Source
"One of my rules is 'Go as close to the source as possible.'" For Starship wall thickness, talked directly to the welders, not the executives. Asked what they thought was safe. "Let's try 4 millimeters." It worked.

## FEEDBACK CULTURE

### Physics Over Feelings
"Physics does not care about hurt feelings. It cares about whether you got the rocket right."

"I give people hardcore feedback, but I try to criticize the action, not the person. What matters is whether a person has a good feedback loop."

### Bad News Must Be Loud
"All bad news should be given loudly and often. Good news can be said quietly and once."

### Don't Optimize for Being Liked
"It's not your job to make people on your team love you. In fact, that's counterproductive."

"Camaraderie is dangerous. It makes it hard for people to challenge each other's work."

"I think it's a real weakness to want to be liked. A real weakness. And I do not have that."

### Ego Management
"A major failure mode is a high ego-to-ability ratio. If your ego gets too high, you've broken the feedback loop to reality."

"In AI terms, you'll break your reinforcement learning loop. You want a strong RL loop, which means internalizing responsibility and minimizing ego."

"Always be smashing your ego. Do whatever the task is, whether grand or humble."

## INNOVATION CULTURE

### Permission to Fail
"Failure is essentially irrelevant unless it is catastrophic."

"When we had early failures in SpaceX flights, I didn't fire anyone responsible. They were smart and hardworking."

"If you punish people too much for failure, the innovation you get will be incremental. Nobody's going to try anything bold for fear of getting punished."

"Failure is a side effect of iteration. I once told a discouraged engineer, 'If you can't tell me the four ways you fucked something up before you got it right, you weren't the one doing the real work.'"

### Darwinian Evolution of Ideas
"You want to let innovation evolve in a Darwinian way. Don't pick one technology and decide it will win. Let things evolve."

"With innovation, you don't know the path — there's no map. It must be culturally acceptable to make false moves."

### Incentive Structure
"Always look at the incentive structure and ask: 'Is the organization properly incentivizing innovation?' The risk-reward must favor taking bold moves."

## MANIACAL URGENCY

### Time Is the Only Currency
"The only true currency is time." "Everything is measured in terms of time."

### Meetings Are the Enemy
"Excessive meetings are the blight of big companies. Get rid of all large meetings, unless you're certain they're providing value to the whole audience."

"Walk out of a meeting or drop off a call as soon as it is obvious you aren't adding value. It is not rude to leave; it is rude to make someone stay and waste their time."

### Speed as Strategy
"The best offense and defense is speed. The SR-71 Blackbird was never shot down — not once. Over three thousand missiles were shot at it and none hit. All it did was go faster."

"Speed of innovation is what matters. If your rate of innovation is high, you don't need to worry about IP protection."

### The Surge Model
For critical problems, mobilize everyone. 24-hour check-ins. Run The Algorithm daily. xAI built Colossus (100K GPUs) in 122 days using this approach — 4 shifts, 24/7 cabling, Elon sleeping in the data center.

### Set Aggressive Timelines
"There's a 'law of gaseous expansion' for schedules. Whatever time you set, it's not going to be less than that."

"When I cite a schedule, it is actually the schedule I think is true. It's not some fake schedule. It may be delusional — that's possible — but it's never knowingly fake."

"I do have a habit of being optimistic with schedules. I may be a little optimistic, but I always deliver."

## COMMUNICATION

### Simple Language
"Don't use acronyms or nonsense words. Excessive use of made-up acronyms is a significant impediment to communication."

"People don't want to seem dumb in a meeting, so they just sit there in ignorance."

"The most simple, straightforward, low-ego terms are generally best."

### Skip Levels
"Whenever there are problems to solve, don't just meet with your managers. Do a skip level, where you meet with the level right below."

### Physically Move to the Problem
"Physically go to where the problem is, immediately." Don't manage from a screen. Go touch the hardware. See the error. Talk to the person doing the actual work.

# The 69 Core Musk Methods

These are the fundamental ideas that make Elon and his companies successful, organized into actionable clusters.

## THINKING METHODS

### 1. You are capable of more than you think
Most people self-limit their ability to learn. "It's pretty straightforward — just read books and talk to people." Elon taught himself rocket science from library books and expert conversations. No aerospace degree.
**When to apply**: When someone says "I can't" or "I don't know enough."

### 2. Ordinary people can choose to be extraordinary
"You can choose to be not ordinary. It's possible for ordinary people to choose to be extraordinary." It's a choice, not an innate trait.

### 3. Teach yourself anything — read widely, talk to experts
"I was in rocketry self-taught. I just read a lot of books and talked to a lot of people." Data rate of reading >> speaking. Read the encyclopedia. Play Civilization for the tech tree mental model. Knowledge is a semantic tree — learn the trunk before the leaves.

### 4. Assume you're wrong — aspire to be less wrong
"It's OK to be wrong. Just don't be confident and wrong." Believe things proportionate to the evidence. Wishful thinking causes most mistakes. Always assume you're losing even when you might be winning.

### 5. Internalize responsibility
"I am CEO because I feel responsible, not because it's best for my quality of life." CEOs get a distillation of the worst problems. If there's a crisis, sleep on the factory floor.

### 18. Reason from fundamentals, not from what others are doing
First-principles thinking vs reasoning by analogy. "It's always been done this way" is a ridiculous way to think. Break things down to foundational truths and build up from there. Most of life can use analogy; for important things, use first principles.

### 19. "The magic-wand number"
Imagine the theoretically perfect arrangement of atoms. What would it cost if manufacturing were free? That sets the floor. For rockets, raw materials were 1-2% of the final cost — meaning 98% was manufacturing inefficiency.

### 20. "Know the idiot index"
Ratio of finished product cost to raw material cost. "If a part costs $13,000 but the steel in it costs $200, the idiot index is very high." Every engineer must know the best and worst parts in their systems by this metric at all times.

### 25. The only fixed laws are the laws of physics
"Physics is law. Everything else is a recommendation. I've met many people who can break the laws of man, but never anyone who could break the laws of physics."

### 47. Good taste is learnable
Train yourself to notice what makes something beautiful. Obsess over the details of quality.

## THE ALGORITHM (Steps 21-29)

### 21. The Algorithm: Question → Delete → Simplify → Accelerate → Automate
The five-step process. **ORDER MATTERS.** Elon has personally made the mistake of going backward on all five steps. Tesla automated, accelerated, and simplified things that should have been deleted entirely.

**Step 1: Make your requirements less dumb**
- "Your requirements are definitely dumb. It doesn't matter who gave them to you."
- Requirements from smart people are the most dangerous — you're less likely to question them.
- Every requirement must come from a named person, not a department.
- Many requirements were created by interns who no longer work there.

**Step 2: Try very hard to delete the part or process**
- "If you're not adding deleted things back in 10% of the time, you're not deleting enough."
- People overcorrect after painful deletions. They add "just in case" items back.
- "We are on a DELETION RAMPAGE!! Nothing is sacred."
- Mass is recursive in rockets — 1 extra ton begets ~1.8 extra tons.

**Step 3: Simplify or optimize**
- "The most common mistake of smart engineers is to optimize a thing that should not exist."
- This is step THREE, not step ONE. Everyone is trained to answer the question in front of them. School taught convergent logic — you can't tell the professor "your question is dumb."
- Simplicity creates both reliability and low cost.

**Step 4: Accelerate cycle time**
- Only AFTER you've questioned, deleted, and simplified.
- "If you're digging your grave, don't dig it faster. Stop digging."
- You can always go faster once direction is right.

**Step 5: Automate**
- The LAST step, not the first.
- Tesla's big mistake: automating every step too early. Had to tear hundreds of expensive robots out. Cut a hole in the building wall to remove equipment.
- Always wait until after design is stabilized before automating.

### 22. For critical items, 24-hour check-ins
Run The Algorithm daily on critical path items. What was yesterday's progress? What blocked us? What's the plan for the next 24 hours?

### 24. All requirements should be treated as recommendations
No requirement is sacred. Question every single one. The person who created it must take responsibility for it.

### 26. The best part is no part; the best process is no process
Fewer components = fewer things to buy, fewer things that can go wrong. Tesla Model 3 body line went from 5,000 to 7,000 cars/week by REMOVING things.

### 27. Simplicity creates both reliability and low cost
"Never use a cruise missile to kill a fly; just use a flyswatter." Lines of code is not a figure of merit — award 1 point for adding, 2 for deleting.

### 28. Find the design necessity of every part and every process
Battery fiberglass mat story: one team said it was for fire safety, the other said noise. Neither needed it. Deleted the part, deleted $2M of robotics.

### 29. Overdelete and add back the absolutely necessary
"We're deliberately going to delete more than we should. Some things we'll add back. That's the right approach."

## SPEED METHODS

### 32. A maniacal sense of urgency is our operating principle
This isn't motivational rhetoric — it's an operating system. Every day you're slower is revenue missed.

### 33. A factory at 2x speed equals two factories
"We can't compete with big car companies in size, so we must do so with intelligence and agility." Think: how can each factory produce what normally requires 5-10 factories?

### 34. Attack the bottleneck
"If you have 9,999 things working and one that isn't, that one sets the overall production rate." The production line moves as fast as the slowest part. Identify it. Fix it. Move to the next bottleneck.

### 35. You'll move as fast as your least-lucky supplier
"Any natural disaster you care to name has happened to our suppliers. A factory burned down. An earthquake. A tsunami. A shoot-out at the Mexican border delayed trunk carpet."

### 36. Do things in parallel
"Avoid serialized dependencies." Many things have gestation periods — have them all gestating simultaneously. PayPal built software while simultaneously closing vendor deals. xAI built Colossus (100K GPUs) in 122 days by renting generators, chillers, and running cabling crews 24/7 in parallel.

### 37. Give teams one key metric
"Video games without a score are boring." Each team needs a clear number to optimize.

### 39. Speed of innovation is what matters
"The real way you achieve IP protection is by innovating fast. If your rate of innovation is high, other companies will be copying what you did years ago."

### 40. Beat competitors on speed, quality, and cost
Not anticompetitive behavior. Win by being genuinely better and faster.

### 41. Test the absurd — "What would it take?"
When told something is impossible: "What would it take to make it possible?" Breaks the mental block.

## PEOPLE METHODS

### 42. Money is not the constraint — exceptional engineers are
"If there was a factory producing excellent engineers, that would be true [that money could solve it]. Except this factory doesn't exist."

### 43. Get everyone thinking like the chief engineer
They need to understand the system at a high level to know when they're making a bad optimization.

### 44. Get a clear, direct feedback loop with reality
"A major failure mode is a high ego-to-ability ratio." In AI terms: don't break your reinforcement learning loop.

### 45. Always be smashing your ego — ensure ability > ego
"Do whatever the task is, whether grand or humble." Technical managers must spend 20% of time doing the actual work.

### 46. Ask: "Is this effort resulting in a better product?"
If not, stop. Simple filter.

### 48. Physics doesn't care about hurt feelings — make the rocket fly
"I give people hardcore feedback, but I try to criticize the action, not the person." All bad news should be loud and frequent.

### 49. Empathy is not an asset (in certain contexts)
"I think it's a real weakness to want to be liked. A real weakness. And I do not have that." Camaraderie is dangerous when it prevents challenging each other's work.

### 50. Use simple, clear, humble terms
"Don't use acronyms or nonsense words." Excessive acronyms are a significant impediment. Made-up glossaries are corporate disease.

### 51. Go directly to the source of information
Skip the chain of command. Talk to the welder about wall thickness, not the VP. "Communication should travel via the shortest path necessary."

### 52. When hiring, look for evidence of exceptional ability
"Tell me the story of your career and the tougher problems you dealt with." The person who actually solved the problem knows the details. The pretender doesn't.

### 53. Combine engineering and financial fluency
"One reason SpaceX could move so quickly is I made both the engineering and spending decisions in one brain."

### 54. To truly lead the product, lead the company
"I encounter CEOs who don't know the details of their technology, and that's ridiculous to me."

### 55. Lead from the front — sleep on the factory floor
Not in the conference room where nobody can see you. On the floor, where the team sees you at 4am. "If the CEO is willing to take that level of pain, I can do it too."

### 56. Physically move to wherever the problem is
"Go as close to the source as possible." Don't manage from an ivory tower. Napoleon's armies fought best wherever Napoleon was.

### 57. All bad news loudly and often; good news quietly and once
Creates a culture where problems surface fast.

## COURAGE METHODS

### 6-8. Building useful things
"If we don't make stuff, there is no stuff." Creating products creates wealth. A useful life is worth having lived.

### 9-12. Purpose and direction
Don't aspire to glory — aspire to work. Take actions that increase odds of a good future. Work on what is just becoming possible.

### 13-17. Starting
Don't wait for the world to want it. Build what no one else is building. Allies assemble around you. Prototypes are proof. Start somewhere, question assumptions, adapt to reality.

### 30-31. Bold action
Push for radical breakthroughs. Be proactive — set strategy, don't react.

### 58. Failure is irrelevant unless catastrophic
Permission to fail, not permission to be mediocre. "If you can't tell me four ways you fucked something up before you got it right, you weren't doing the real work."

### 59. Fear of failure is the biggest cause of failure
"If failure is not an option, it's going to result in extremely conservative choices."

### 60. Feel the fear and do it anyway
"I feel fear quite strongly. But when something is important enough, you do it in spite of fear." Fatalism helps — accept the probabilities, and fear diminishes.

### 61. Double down — push your chips back in
After selling Zip2 for $22M, put almost all of it into X.com/PayPal. After PayPal sold for $170M, put it into SpaceX and Tesla. "I'm not good at sitting back."

### 62-63. Work like hell and take the pain
"80-100 hour weeks, every week." The simple math: at 100 hrs/week vs someone else's 50, you get twice as much done. "My way of dealing with mental problems is to make sure you really care about what you're doing — and take the pain."

### 64-66. Persist through tragedy
"We should not be afraid of doing something important just because some amount of tragedy is likely to occur." When something is important enough, do it even when odds are against you. "Don't ever give up. You'd have to be dead or completely incapacitated."

### 67-69. Mindset
Play life like a game. Go ultra hardcore. Humor is a differentiator.
